The Seed Rate Calculator is a useful experimental instrument used to allow the farmers agronomists and seed technologists to decide how much seed they should sow to a field to achieve a desired plant population. The calculator then works with the most significant agronomic variables - target plant population per square meter thousand gram weight of germination percentage field losses and crop area thousand hectares - and converts them into understandable measurements: sowing rate kilograms per hectare of seeds per square meter of seeds per hectare and tonnes of seed mass needed to cover your territory. It also works out effective germination and adjusted loss rates in such a way that you can observe the effect of seed quality and field conditions on the outcome.

Why this is important: a small miscalculation in seed rate will result in a either a loss of money through excess seeding or a loss of revenue through underseeding. The calculator provides you with an evidence based seeding plan which you can trust with a combination of simple algebra and field tested corrections. The tool takes into consideration the size of the seed by weight of a thousand grains and the real world factors such as reduced germination and field losses to ensure your sowing recommendations are the same as what actually comes out of the field.

Calculate the number of tonnes to buy at an approximation of the nearest tonne to budget correctly to calculate the kilograms of your drill that are required to calibrate planters and to compare the amount of seed you require between different crops and treatments using the calculator. Its outputs are converted into common units such that you can directly input the numbers in planter settings pre supply orders or farm management records.

What is a seed rate

This is the quantity of seed sown on a hectare of land that is measured in kilograms per hectare seeds on hectare or in some cases the number of seeds per linear meter of row. Fundamentally seed rate is a number of planning. It is the number of seeds to place in the soil that will result in the required plant population after the germination emergence and early survival are factors are considered.

Seed rate can be associated with three related layers that can be considered. The initial one is biological: the seeds vary in size and shape and their potential to germinate and develop. The mass of one thousand grains that are converted to grams is known as the average mass of one thousand grains and this is a direct measure of seed size. Thousand grain weight is less, and therefore there are fewer seeds per kilogram hence kg/hectare answers, and the seeds per hectare must react on this. The second level is environmental: soil moisture seedbed quality disease pests and mechanical damage decrease the percentage of the seeds germinating into plants. We commonly measure these effects using two inputs: germination percentage in the lab based on a laboratory germination experiment and field loss percentage which is an estimate of the loss after seeding. The third level is management: planting depth, sowing method row spacing and seed treatments influence the number of seeds that are converted into plants. An example is that when drilling penetrates with a firm seedbed at the right depth this makes the establishment more than when the shallow broadcasts the sewerange loosely.

Units matter. There are many standard units that are used by farmers and advisors depending on the task. The usefulness of the seeds per square meter and seeds per hectare is in agronomy and planning between field sizes. Kilograms per hectare are helpful in purchasing seed and setup seed drills. The common unit used to calibrate the planters and seed drills is the number of seeds per linear meter of row. An effective calculator will have all these units connected together so that when one input is changed all the outputs will be changed.

Practically farmers do not select a seed rate initially and singly. Their target population of plants is determined by variety recommendation soil type and preferred stand. Then they determine the seed rate which will reach that target based on the weight of the seeds and their anticipated losses on establishment. The seed rate then is a working figure derived through biological target. This is the reason why contemporary calculators are requesting you to enter target plants per square meter instead of just requesting you to type in a figure of kg per hectare.

Lastly is seed rate which is crop specific and region specific. The practice that is effective with small seeded grain crops such as wheat is not the same with row crops such as maize soybean or sunflower. The calculator must thus be able to be flexible in its inputs to capture the crop specific thousand-gram weight recommended target populations and local expectations regarding germination and losses. When you enter the calculator you are converting a biological target to an operating plan which you can purchase seed, put into your planter and watch when it begins to grow.

How to calculate seed rate manually

It is easy to work out the seed rate manually, when you arithmetically compute it in the only sequence. The formulas are given in full derivation below and an example worked out in detail with step by step arithmetic operation to ensure you can check all the steps.

Step 1 Define the inputs

  • Target population T in plants per square meter (plants/m2)
  • Thousand grain weight TGW in grams per 1000 seeds (g)
  • Germination percentage G as percent (for example 90 for ninety percent)
  • Field losses percentage L as percent (for example 10 for ten percent)
  • Area A in hectares when you want total seed tonnage

Step 2 Calculate the effective establishment fraction

Determine percent inputs as fractions. The ratio of germination to 100 is G/100. Loss fraction Field survival F = (100- L)/100. Divide the fraction of the two to obtain the effective establishment fraction E.

Algebraically:

E = (G / 100) × (1 − L / 100)

Step 3 Seeds required per square meter

Seeds per square meter S_m2 is the target population divided by the effective establishment fraction.

S_m2 = T / E

Step 4 Seeds per hectare

Seeds per hectare S_ha = S_m2 × 10,000 because one hectare contains 10,000 square meters.

S_ha = S_m2 × 10,000

Step 5 Convert seeds per hectare to kg per hectare

The thousand grain weight TGW is the weight of 1000 grains of seeds. Grams per seed = TGW / 1000. So grams per hectare = S_ha x (TGW / 1000). Divide into kilomograms. That gives:

kg per hectare = S_ha × TGW / 1,000,000

Combined formula

The algebra can be combined to create a compact closed form which can be easily written by hand. Starting from S_m2 = T / ( (G / 100) x (1 [?] L / 100) ) and going on to kg per hectare you can simplify constants. The combined formula is:

kg per hectare = 100 × T × TGW / (G × (100 − L))

At this point, we will present a more detailed numerical example and will calculate each step of the operation at a time to prevent arithmetic errors.

Worked example

Suppose T = 250 plants/m 2 TGW = 40Gm germination G = 90 percent and elective field losses L = 10 percent. We shall work out number of seeds per square meter seeds per hectare kg per hectare and overall seed of a 50 hectare field.

Convert percents to fractions step by step:

G divided by 100 = 90 / 100 = 0.90.

L divided by 100 = 10 / 100 = 0.10.

Effective establishment fraction E = G fraction × (1 minus L fraction)

Compute (1 minus L fraction) = 1 − 0.10 = 0.90.

Now multiply G fraction by (1 minus L fraction):

E = 0.90 × 0.90.

Multiply 0.90 × 0.90 digit by digit: 9 × 9 = 81 so 0.90 × 0.90 = 0.8100 interpreted as 0.81.

So the effective establishment fraction E = 0.81.

Now compute seeds per square meter S_m2:

S_m2 = T / E = 250 / 0.81.

Do the division carefully. 0.81 goes into 250 how many times? You can compute 250 divided by 0.81 by converting to 25000 divided by 81 which is easier for hand arithmetic.

250 ÷ 0.81 = 25000 ÷ 81.

Now divide 81 into 25000:

81 times 308 = 81 × 300 + 81 × 8 = 24,300 + 648 = 24,948.

81 times 309 = 24,948 + 81 = 25,029 which is greater than 25,000. So the integer part is 308 remainder 25,000 − 24,948 = 52.

So 25000 ÷ 81 = 308 and remainder 52. As decimal fraction remainder 52 gives next digits 520 ÷ 81 = 6 remainder 520 − 486 = 34. Next digit 340 ÷ 81 = 4 remainder 340 − 324 = 16 etc. The result rounded to three decimals is 308.642.

Therefore seeds per square meter S_m2 ≈ 308.642 seeds per m2.

Now seeds per hectare S_ha = S_m2 × 10,000.

S_ha = 308.642 × 10,000 = 3,086,420 seeds per hectare (rounding to whole seeds).

Now convert seeds per hectare to kg per hectare:

kg per hectare = S_ha × TGW / 1,000,000.

Substitute values: S_ha = 3,086,420 TGW = 40 g.

Compute S_ha × TGW = 3,086,420 × 40.

3,086,420 × 4 = 12,345,680. Multiply by 10 to get times 40 so result = 123,456,800 grams per hectare.

Divide grams by 1, 000: 123,456,800/1, 000= 123,456.8 kilograms. Divide by further 1,000 since the conversion factor of the original formula is 1,000,000 hence better step: grams to kilograms = divide by 1,000 to give 123,456.8 kg though we used TGW and S_ha conversion; so technically we need to use 1,000,000 as the conversion factor instead of 1,000 so attention, is necessary.

Following the compact formula kg per hectare = S_ha × TGW / 1,000,000 so compute 3,086,420 × 40 ÷ 1,000,000 = 123,456,800 ÷ 1,000,000 = 123.4568 kg per hectare.

So kg per hectare = 123.4568 kg/ha.

Finally for a 50 hectare field total seed needed in kg = kg per hectare × area.

Total kg = 123.4568 × 50.

Compute 123.4568 × 50 = 123.4568 × (5 × 10) = (123.4568 × 5) × 10.

123.4568 × 5 = 617.284 so × 10 gives 6,172.84 kg.

Convert to tonnes by dividing by 1,000: 6,172.84 ÷ 1,000 = 6.17284 tonnes.

Summary of numeric results in this example:

  • Seeds per m2 ≈ 308.642
  • Seeds per hectare ≈ 3,086,420
  • kg per hectare ≈ 123.4568
  • Total seed for 50 ha ≈ 6,172.84 kg or 6.17284 tonnes

This is a step wise procedure that will allow you to confirm every step. In case any input is different like the germination or TGW reiterates the same sequence and recalculates. Do the math by hand watch the fraction conversions and unit conversions so that you do not make mistakes.

How to use our seed rate calculator

Our seed rate calculator translates the steps in the above to a user friendly interface and hence you can get results instantly with the least probability of error in the arithmetic. A field by field description of the work each control performs and what it is good practice to enter to ensure good operation is given below.

Target population plants per m2

Key in the number of plants you desire to be established at the rate of one per square meter when they have emerged and survived the initial few weeks. This is a choice of agronomy depending on the recommendations of variety plant architecture yield target and row spacing. In case of small grain cereals this may range between 150 to 300 plants/m2 depending on the variety and local practice. In the case of wide row crops such as maize this can be much less but input as plants per m2 in the calculator and make sure to keep the calculations straight at plants per hectare should you need it.

Thousand grain weight TGW in grams

Measure or use the TGW that is being supplied by the seed supplier. To measure we will take a representative sample of seeds whereby we get 1000 seeds and weigh them using a precision scale and note the weight in grams. Assuming that it is not convenient to count 1000 seeds, count 100 seeds multiply weight by 10 or count 500 seeds multiply by 2. In case there is the use of the seed coating or inoculant use the TGW of the seed that has been coated since the coating will add additional weight and will raise the rate of sowing in kg per hectare.

Germination percentage

Enter germination of a recent test, under normal conditions or use lot germination on your seed tag. Insert percentage without percent sign where numeric values are required, e.g. type in 90, which is ninety percent. In case you feel that the seed store test is being too optimistic, take a pessimistic value or repeat a germination test to get a more accurate result. The calculator does not consider this as a field emergence, but as a lab germination rate; field losses are included as an input to the calculator.

Field losses percentage

Field losses consider anything to diminish emergence subsequent to you planting the seed: birds rodents disease insects crust drought mechanical damages and improper planting depth. Insert the percentage lost since sowing; 0 indicates no losses and 20 indicates that one out of every five sown seeds that failed to germinate. Use a small conservative buffer when it is uncertain whether there will be some pressure or not, or when it is uncertain whether there may be some pests around.

Crop area in hectares

Divide the area to be sown by hectares to determine the total tonnage of the seed. It divides kg per hectare with crop area and then changes to tonnes in case you need it to get purchase amounts to the nearest 0.01 tonne should you wish.

What the calculator returns

  • Sowing rate kg per hectare The recommended mass of seed to sow per hectare after adjusting for seed weight germination and field losses.
  • Seed tonnage needed Total mass of seed required for the area you entered expressed in tonnes.
  • Seeds sown seeds per m2 The number of seeds you will actually broadcast or drill per square meter to achieve the target.
  • Seeds per hectare The scaled up seed count for planning and quality control.
  • Total seeds needed For large scale planning this is expressed in millions or raw counts depending on the area.
  • Effective germination The calculator also shows the effective germination upon adding lab germination and field gains so that you can observe the difference between the lab value and that one to expect in the field.

Advanced usage and tips

If you use seed treated with fungicide inoculant or polymer coating measure the TGW on the treated seed. Coatings can add 5 to 20 percent to seed mass and failing to account for them produces underestimates of seeds per kilogram. For planter calibration you will often need seeds per linear meter of row rather than kg per hectare. Convert seeds per m2 into seeds per meter of row by multiplying seeds per m2 by row spacing in meters. For example if seeds per m2 = 308 and row spacing = 0.15 meters then seeds per meter of row = 308 × 0.15 = 46.2 seeds per meter.

Planter calibration checklist

  • Check TGW for treated seed
  • Confirm germination test is recent
  • Estimate field losses conservatively if conditions are uncertain
  • Use the seeds per meter row conversion to set meters of seed per minute on your planter
  • Run a field check at emergence and compare actual plant population to your target then adjust next pass if necessary

Common pitfalls and how the calculator helps

A common mistake is to buy seed using kg per hectare without checking seed size. Two seed lots with the same kg per hectare may contain very different numbers of seeds. The calculator helps by linking seed mass to seed counts using TGW so you can compare lots on seeds per hectare rather than just weight. Another common mistake is ignoring field losses. The calculator makes the difference explicit by computing effective germination so you can see the math behind the recommended kg per hectare.

Using the calculator saves time reduces risk and creates a clear audit trail between your agronomic targets and the operational numbers used by the planter and grain store.

Difference between seed rate and planting density

People often use the terms seed rate and planting density interchangeably but they refer to different planning concepts. Understanding the difference is essential to making the right agronomic decisions and choosing the correct inputs for the calculator.

Planting density or target plant population is the biological objective expressed as plants per square meter or plants per hectare. It is an agronomic decision and depends on the crop variety growth habit yield target and the production system. For example a breeder or extension guide might recommend 250 plants per m2 for a particular wheat variety in your region under irrigated conditions. That number reflects the desired number of individual plants that will capture light intercept the soil surface and create canopy architecture ideal for yield.

seed rate The operation figure that informs you of the quantity of seed to put in the ground to generate that density of planting. Seed rate is given as kg/ha kg/m 2 kg/m 1 total tons of seeds per 1m 2 area. The seed rate is calculated based on the planting density taking into consideration the seed size seed viability and post sowing loss. In short seed rate answers the question how many seeds must I plant so that, after nature and management, I will have the number of living plants that I desire at the planting density I desire?

The association in this case is directional. Planting density is a target agronomic and seed rate is a target operational. Planting density will be misdirected by getting the seed rate incorrect. On the other hand you can plant the right density of plants but you miss the right target density of the crop system and have an inappropriate stand in terms of yield and resource utilization.

Why the distinction matters for management

When calibrating a planter you adjust it to plant a cut of seed in hectares or a quantity of seeds in inches. The calibrator is unaware of your biological target that it is acting upon it simply provides operational input. In order to complete the loop you must transform the biological target to the operating number. That is what the calculator does.

Take a real world example. Suppose you desire 250 plants per m 2 and you are working with a seed lot having TGW of 40 g germination of 90 percent and field losses of 10 percent. The calculation of seed rate in the previous calculation revealed that the rate was approximately 123.5 kg/ha. With a rule of thumb, like 150 kg/ha, and not measuring TGW and germination would result in an actual plant population of 20 percent low or 20 percent high depending on the lot of seed and field conditions. The difference between target and rate averts such a mismatch.

When you might adjust planting density rather than seed rate

In other cases agronomic strategy may require density modification of planting itself. Environments with high yield potential could respond better to high planting density to maximize the intercepted radiation, whereas stress prone environments could be better served through low densities to minimize competition as water becomes limited. In them the agronomist modifies target population of plants on purpose and subsequently recalculates seed rate. The calculator aids in this iterative process since it allows you to test scenarios very fast.

How seed treatments fit in

A similar argument is that seed treatments, e.g. coating or inoculants, alter the mass of individual seeds that they influence the transformation of seed number to kg. A comparison of treated and untreated lots on a kg per hectare basis will give a false impression of the number of seeds delivered. The TGW-calculated seed-treated calculator maintains the appropriate density and seed rate of the planting since the rate of seed takes into consideration the coating.

Practical takeaway

The planting density should always be started with. Enter that biological target in the calculator to create an operational seed rate which includes TGW germination and field losses. Check planter settings with the seeds per meter conversion and assure populations at emergence. The distinction that comes between the planting density and seed rate is what enables you to dictate the results of the fields rather than using the rule of thumb.

FAQs

Is thousand grain weight the same as seed weight per kilogram?

Thousand grain weight TGW is a metric of the mass of a thousand grains of the grains of the number of seeds. It is not exactly equal to the weight of a kilogram of seed but rather it is directly related. Knowing TGW you may know how many seeds in kilogram since seeds per kilogram = 1,000,000/ TGW when TGW is expressed in grams. As an example when TGW = 40 g then seeds per kilogram = 1,000,000 / 40 = 25,000 seeds per kilogram. The TGW is useful since it brings seed size to a normalized state, and you can step up and down seed counts as well as seed mass.

How do seed coatings and treatments change calculations?

Coatings change the mass per seed and therefore increase TGW. If you measure TGW on untreated seed but then plan to sow treated seed your kg per hectare calculation will be wrong because treated seed is heavier so fewer seeds are contained in a kilogram. To avoid that mistake always measure TGW on the actual seed material you will sow including any coatings or inoculants. If that is not possible ask your supplier for treated seed TGW.

How accurate are germination tests and how often should I run them?

Germination tests are standardized and tend to work reasonably well provided they are done properly on representative samples. But lot heterogeneity storage and manipulation may alter the germination with time. Conduct a germination test prior to large purchases and control again prior to sowing when seed has been kept in variable conditions several months. In the case of critical crops an estimate would be more reliable when a replicate test of 100 seed that is conducted under controlled conditions and repeated on two or three subsamples is used.

How should I estimate field losses?

The most difficult input to define is field losses that are influenced by local pest pressure soil moisture emergence problems and mechanical problems. As a point of reference, use the historical local experience of the crop and sowing window. When dealing with a high risk environment include a buffer. In a low risk irrigated seedbed you might have 5 to 10 percent and in a hot dry broadcast sowing you might have 20 percent or more. The calculator also allows you to experiment with conditions to try low medium and high loss assumptions to find out how sensitive your kg per hectare recommendation is.

Can I compensate for low germination by increasing seed rate?

Yes higher seed rate overcomes low germination but again there is a limit. The cost doubles with doubling the seed rate and may lead to competition should a significant portion of the seed actually take hold. Poor germination can be a symptom of either bad seed health or bad storage conditions and purchasing a better seed or fixing storage conditions is usually cheaper. Enter the values into the calculator to find out the cost that is necessary to achieve your target population of plant with a low germination lot and compare that to the cost of changing the seed lot.

What is the difference between seeds per hectare and seeds per meter of row and how do I convert?

Hectare count of seeds per hectare is a field-scale count that is used in field level planning and to purchase seeds. The number of seeds per meter of row is an example of how you set planter meters which causes the machine to deposit the correct amount of seeds per row. Divide the number of seeds per m2 by the row spacing in meters to get the number of seeds per meter of row. An example is that, when seed per m2 = 308 and row spacing = 0.15 m then seed per meter = 308 x 0.15 = 46.2 seeds per meter. Divide that number into the number of meters per minute that planters should put across at your ground speed.

How much should I trust a single calculation for final purchase decisions?

One computation is a good beginning but one you must consider as being a part of a planning process. Check TGW on a representative sample update germination when a long-term stock lot has been put up and use conservative field loss assumptions to deal with unknown risk. When you sow take counts of emergence and feed back observed populations into your plans on later fields. The calculator hastens the scenario testing but alone it cannot take the place of simple quality control measures like germination tests and field checks.

Does seed moisture content affect TGW?

Yes seed moisture affects weight. TGW should be measured at standard moisture conditions or at least the moisture content should be recorded. If seed is wetter than normal TGW will be higher and the kg per hectare will be overestimated for seed delivered at normal moisture. For high precision purchases ask your seed lab to report moisture alongside TGW.
